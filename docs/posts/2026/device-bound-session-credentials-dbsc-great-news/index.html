Device Bound Session Credentials: Great news. Not a silver bullet.

Posted on May 29, 2026
#cookies #red #ttp

Great news. A few weeks ago Google announced that Device Bound Session Credentials, or DBSC, are now publicly available starting with Chrome 146 on Windows. And a few days ago there was a follow-up post that DBSC is now enabled by default for Google Workspace users in Chrome for Windows.

I remember there were some early experiments with binding cookies to TLS sessions, but somehow that never really went anywhere. In contrast, DBSC is now live and in production use.

The Threat: Credential Stealers

One trick that is popular to get access to cookies is using Chrome’s remote debugging port to read all browser cookies and then exfiltrate, and use them. I still feel proud that Google mentioned my blog in their post about this two years back.

There are other, similar tricks that credential-stealing malware use, but the business model usually depends on copying the cookies off the compromised machines and then selling them.

DBSC mitigates this by requiring possession of a non-exportable private device key. That private key is managed by the TPM and the browser signs a server challenge roughly every five minutes to prove possession.

So, even if an adversary steals and copies cookies, they cannot be used from another device.

Is DBSC a silver bullet?

No, certainly not. But it’s a very meaningful improvement!

Live Access and Lateral Movement

Attackers can still use cookies “live” when they have compromised a machine. This is a typical scenario and daily bread and butter for red teams. DBSC mitigates session cookie theft and replay. It does not by itself prevent usage when an attacker has access to the victim’s computer.

In short, the attacker has to maintain presence.

Also, worth highlighting that malware running on a compromised host can likely de-auth, and then hijack the registration and refresh using their own key! The spec points this out as a non-goal for mitigation, and notes that an attacker controlling the user agent at registration time can bind the session to a key of their own (non-TPM bound key).

Which brings us to proxies and phishing sites.

Phishing Proxies

DBSC won’t stop a phishing proxy either. But proxies need to adapt to “support” DBSC and hook the appropriate APIs correctly to take control. However, they also have to remain within the limits of DBSC (e.g. refresh requirements,…).

Conceptually, a DBSC-aware phishing proxy would perform the binding itself. It intercepts the Secure-Session-Registration header, then registers its own software key (but could also be from its own TPM), and then also answers all future refresh challenges.

The catch is that phishing kits will need to actively do this. So, we should expect phishing proxies to adapt accordingly. I’m not maintaining KoiPhish anymore, but it should be possible to add such a feature to see if this works.

WebAuthN is the already existing mitigation for that kind of attack if deployed correctly.

XSS and CSRF

Finally, this is obvious, but I figured it is worth mentioning that attacks like XSS or CSRF will similarly continue to work.

Conclusion

Stealing cookies from machines during red team operations is one of the attacks that I have been using for more than 12 years. Going back to red teaming at Microsoft Azure where I even wrote special Cookie Monster tooling for it.

DBSC is a very positive improvement and it throws a wrench into cookie export, offline replay, and credential stealer workflows. And real-world threat actors use credential stealers commonly and sell information on the dark web. So, seeing this being added to Chrome (and hopefully other browsers) is great news.

It remains critical to continue adopting phishing resistant WebAuthN, and move away from less-secure MFA protocols.

WebAuthN and DBSC complement each other well.

Kudos to the Google team for shipping this!
